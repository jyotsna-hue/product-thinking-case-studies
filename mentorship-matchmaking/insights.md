# Key Insights

## 1. The Core Problem is Discovery, Not Willingness

The survey results showed that most students actively try to seek mentorship during placements. However, they struggle to identify and reach the right alumni mentors.

Students are willing to ask for help, but the discovery process is inefficient and fragmented.

This suggests that the primary opportunity lies in **improving mentor discovery rather than building complex mentorship management tools.**

---

## 2. Current Solutions Are Unstructured

Students currently rely on:

- Cold LinkedIn messages
- Informal introductions through seniors
- Searching alumni groups

These approaches are inconsistent and depend heavily on chance.

A structured discovery system could significantly reduce the effort required to find relevant mentors.

---

## 3. Speed of Discovery Matters

Students preparing for placements often operate under time pressure.

A swipe-based exploration interface allows users to quickly evaluate mentors without navigating large directories or searching through LinkedIn profiles.

Reducing friction in the discovery phase increases the likelihood that students will reach out for mentorship.

---

## 4. The MVP Should Focus on a Single Core Problem

The analysis shows that the product should initially focus on solving **one key problem: discovering relevant mentors quickly.**

Features such as messaging systems, scheduling, or rating systems can be introduced later once the discovery experience proves valuable.

Focusing on a single core interaction improves the chances of achieving **early product validation.**

---

## Final Takeaway

This exploration highlights how a seemingly simple student problem can reveal an opportunity for a structured product experience.

By focusing on discovery, validating the problem through user input, and prioritizing lightweight interactions, the mentorship platform can address a real friction students face during placements.
